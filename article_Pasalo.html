Understanding Pasalo: A Guide for Owners and Potential Buyers

 A pasalo arrangement, or assumption of mortgage, offers a unique way for an property buyer (often facing financial constraints) to transfer their property’s mortgage to a new buyer. This approach can help the owner exit their mortgage while giving the new buyer an affordable pathway to homeownership. However, both parties must understand the process, risks, and legal nuances to avoid future complications. This comprehensive guide will discuss the essential factors each party should consider before engaging in a pasalo transaction.

Part 1: Considerations for the Owner

As the owner, deciding to transfer a mortgage to another party can be a solution for mitigating financial challenges. However, an immediate transfer of ownership through a Deed of Absolute Sale (DOAS), without repaying the mortgage, involves risks and key considerations.

Why Pasalo?

  • Relief from Mortgage Payments: If the owner faces financial difficulties, transferring the mortgage can alleviate the burden without impacting their financial stability further.
  • Retain Equity: If the buyer has already paid a significant portion of the mortgage (e.g., down payment and initial payments), a pasalo allows them to recoup these costs from the new buyer.
  • Faster Process: Compared to a full sale, a pasalo arrangement can expedite the transfer, especially for properties where the mortgage terms are favorable compared to current market rates.

Immediate Risks of Executing a Deed of Absolute Sale (DOAS)

While it’s possible for the owner to execute a DOAS to transfer ownership immediately, this route entails several potential risks:

  • Credit Risk: Despite transferring ownership through a DOAS, the owner remains legally responsible for the mortgage. If the new buyer defaults, the lender may pursue the owner for unpaid amounts, leading to potential credit score impact.
  • Tax Obligations: A DOAS triggers Capital Gains Tax (CGT) and Documentary Stamp Tax (DST), even if the mortgage is not fully paid off. The owner might end up paying these taxes without receiving full compensation if the new buyer defaults on future payments.
  • Loss of Control: Once a DOAS is signed, the owner relinquishes legal rights to the property, making it challenging to reclaim ownership in case of default or issues with the new buyer.

Alternative: Conditional Deed of Sale

A Conditional Deed of Sale (CDOS) is a safer option, allowing the owner to retain ownership until the new buyer fully pays the mortgage. This protects the owner’s interests, avoids premature tax obligations, and mitigates credit risks.

Part 2: Essential Documents and Steps for the Owner

  • Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) or Deed of Assignment: This document outlines the pasalo arrangement, detailing the terms of payment, responsibilities, and conditions that must be met.
  • Authorization from the Lender or Developer: Some lenders require formal consent for a pasalo transaction. Securing authorization helps formalize the arrangement and avoid contract breaches.
  • Payment Receipts and Mortgage Documents: Providing a history of payments assures the new buyer of the mortgage status, while also demonstrating the equity the owner has accumulated.
  • Conditional Agreement or Deed of Absolute Sale: If both parties agree, a CDOS provides a safer route for ownership transfer once the mortgage is paid in full, protecting the buyer legally and financially.

Part 3: Considerations for the New Buyer

When to Consider Pasalo

  • Lower Upfront Cost: Compared to a standard mortgage, a pasalo arrangement often involves a smaller upfront payment to the buyer, making it attractive to buyers with limited cash.
  • Immediate Occupancy: Unlike preselling units, pasalo properties are typically ready for occupancy, providing a faster move-in option.
  • Favorable Mortgage Terms: If the buyer’s mortgage rate is lower than current rates, the new buyer can benefit from the pre-existing loan terms.

Risks for the New Buyer

  • Liability without Formal Loan Assumption: In many pasalo transactions, the lender views only the buyer as the legal borrower. Without the lender’s formal consent, the new buyer could face complications if the owner defaults.
  • Tax and Transfer Costs: Upon full payment of the mortgage, the new buyer is responsible for the Documentary Stamp Tax (DST), Capital Gains Tax (CGT), and other title transfer costs.
  • Condition of the Property: Pasalo properties may need repairs. Conducting a property inspection can reveal any issues that might require investment beyond the purchase price.

Alternatively, a new loan to pay off the mortgage can give the new buyer complete ownership, avoiding complications with loan assumptions and title transfer.
